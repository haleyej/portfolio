Reading:

This summer I decided to read a book every week! I ended up only missing about one week of reading, which considering how much I worked and traveled is pretty good! Here are my reads (mostly in order):

Normal People by Sally Rooney

★★★★★

I read Normal People in two sittings. The hype is real and this book is fantastic – I even convinced my mom to read it.

Invisible Women: Data Bias in a World Designed for Men by Caroline Criado Perez

★★★☆☆

This book was very. . . underwhelming. It touches on lots of important areas – ranging from product design to transportation date to sampling in clinical trials – but jumps from one statistic to another without any real analysis. This book seemed like it just wanted to prove data bias against women is real without deeply engaging with the subject.

Trick Mirror by Jia Tolentino

★★★★★

I first became obsessed with Jia Tolentino's work when I listened to a podcast episode where she talked about using Bitcoin to buy drugs in 2015. Needless to say her essay collection did not disapoint. She writes about everything from marriage to sexual assault on campus to the wellnss industry. If there's one book from this summer I'll definetly re-read, it's this one.

Norwegian Wood by Haruki Murakami

★★★★☆

Murakami masterfully captures feelings of isolation, nostalgia and identity. This is the first Murakami that I've read, and I was instantly captivated by the dream-like quality of his writing. If you appreciate writing as a craft and an author's ability to evoke emotion, you'll love this. If you're more of a plot-driven person, this might not be for you.

In the Shadow of the Ivory Tower: How Universities Are Plundering Our Cities By Davarian L. Baldwin

★★★☆☆

I had heard good things about this book, but it ultimately feel short for me. As someone who's not super familiar with urban planning and zoning laws, this book tried to cover too much too fast.

Shape: The Hidden Geometry of Information, Biology, Strategy, Democracy, and Everything Else by Jordan Ellenberg

★★★★☆

Not to be *that person* but if you can get throug the first ~100-150 pages this book is great. The fantastic and insightful chapters on modeling pandemics, gerrymandering and game theory get bogged down but some well-written but dense geometrics proofs at the beginning.

The Drunkard's Walk: How Randomness Rules Our Lives by Leonard Mlodinow

★★★★★

I loved this book, but I also love statistics so take my opinion with a grain of salt. This is a good mix of the history of statistics and real-world applications of statistics and randomness.

Draft No. 4: On the Writing Process by John McPhee

★★★☆☆

I can't really articulate what it is about this book that didn't do it for me. It was well written, but McPhee's stories from his career and thoughts on the craft of writing weren't super engaging to me.

Cultish: The Language of Fanaticism by Amanda Montell

★★★★☆

This book is fun and a quick read (and lead to many wikipedia rabbit holes). I have to ding it one star because it's not really about linguistiics/language/rhetorical tactics. She does go into that, but it's secondary to the descriptions of the cults she highlights.

The Cult of We: WeWork, Adam Neumann, and the Great Startup Delusion by Eliot Brown & Maureen Farrell

★★★★★

I have a weird fascination with startup culture, so despite this book being 400 pages it was a pretty quick read. The authors do a great job connecting startup and VC culture to broader changes in society.

On Deck:

I realized I went on a big non-fiction kick this summer so I'm hoping to dive back into some liteature.
